The Problem

Why isn't encryption alone enough?

Sertkaya Bilgehan

Alright, I'm up. Bilgehan here. Let me start with a question that sounds a bit odd at first: encryption is good, right? So why isn't it enough? The answer is: encryption solves one problem, but not the most important one. I'll show you what that means over the next three slides. Timing: ~15 seconds, just a transition.

What Does the Padlock Mean?

What does it actually mean?
Most people think: "The site is secure."
What it actually means:
① The connection is encrypted
② The server's identity has been verified
Today we'll show you how that second claim comes about — because that's the hard part.
Okay — when did you last actually pay attention to the padlock in your browser? Probably never, right? And when you did, what did you think? "All good, the site is secure." That's not entirely wrong, but it's not entirely right either. The padlock actually makes two claims at once. First: the connection is encrypted — nobody can eavesdrop. Second: the server has proven it really is who it claims to be. And it's that second claim that's interesting. Encryption alone can't deliver it. That's what PKI is for. But first — why do we need authentication in the first place? Timing: ~1:30 minutes.

The Three Security Goals

Confidentiality
"Nobody eavesdrops."

Solution: Symmetric encryption (AES-GCM — the industry-standard algorithm). Only sender and receiver can read the data.
Integrity
"Nobody tampers."

Solution: MACs (Message Authentication Codes) / AEAD (Authenticated Encryption with Associated Data). Every message carries a cryptographic checksum — any manipulation is detected immediately.
Authenticity
"Are you really you?"

This is where it gets interesting ↓
Encryption and MACs assume we're talking to the right person — but how do we know that?
In IT security there are three classic goals — the CIA triad: Confidentiality, Integrity, Authenticity. Confidentiality means nobody reads along. We solve that with symmetric encryption, like AES. Integrity means nobody secretly alters the data. We solve that with MACs or AEAD cipher modes. But the third goal — authenticity — is where encryption alone breaks down. We can encrypt as strongly as we want, but if we're talking to the wrong server, it's useless. And that's the problem I'm about to show you. Timing: ~1:30 minutes.

Man-in-the-Middle Attack

Alice
you in the browser
Mallory
café Wi-Fi, router, ISP…
Bob
your bank
Encryption alone doesn't help. If Mallory sits between Alice and Bob, and Alice thinks she's talking to Bob — then Alice encrypts her data for Mallory. We need a way to authenticate the server before we trust it with anything.
Imagine you're sitting in a café logging into your online banking. The connection is encrypted — great. But what if the café's Wi-Fi router is run by someone called Mallory? Mallory can position herself between you and the bank. She pretends to be the bank to you, and pretends to be you to the bank. That's a Man-in-the-Middle attack. Your encryption runs with Mallory — not with the bank. Mallory sees everything. This isn't a theoretical attack scenario. It happens in practice — especially on unsecured networks. And the solution? The server needs to be able to prove its identity. Not just claim it — prove it. How that works is what Paulina explains next. Timing: ~1:30 minutes. Then hand over to Paulina.
Part 2

The Solution: PKI

Public Key Infrastructure — Trust, Delegated

Wiegel Paulina

Thanks, Bilgehan. I'm Paulina, and I'll now explain how the problem gets solved. PKI — Public Key Infrastructure — is essentially a system that makes trust scalable. Sounds abstract, but it'll get very concrete very quickly. Timing: ~15 seconds transition.

Delegating Trust

Nobody can personally know ~400 million websites.

→ Instead of knowing every key directly, we trust
a few vetted authorities to vouch for others.

Analogy: Passport

When a police officer abroad accepts your passport, they don't trust you personally — they trust the government that issued and signed it.

Same with PKI: your browser doesn't personally know yourbank.de. It trusts a Certificate Authority (CA) that vouches for yourbank.de — and the CA it does know.

Imagine you had to personally know 400 million websites and manually trust each one's key. That's obviously absurd — no person or system can do that. The solution is to delegate trust. Instead of knowing every website directly, your browser trusts a small group of specially vetted authorities — the Certificate Authorities, or CAs. And these CAs vouch for the websites. The passport analogy makes this very tangible: if you're in Japan and an officer accepts your German passport, they're not trusting you personally — they trust the German government that issued and cryptographically signed that document. Same with PKI: your browser doesn't directly trust the bank — it trusts the CA that vouched for the bank, and the CA is who it knows. Timing: ~2 minutes.

The Chain of Trust

self-signed · offline in HSM (Hardware Security Module)

▼ signs

Well-known Root CAs

  • ISRG Root X1 — Let's Encrypt
  • DigiCert Global Root G2
  • GlobalSign Root CA
  • Sectigo (formerly Comodo)
  • Entrust Root CA

Why three levels?

  • Root key stays offline in an HSM (tamper-proof hardware vault) — unreachable
  • Intermediates handle day-to-day issuance
  • If compromised: revoke only the Intermediate — Root stays safe
Now let's look at the chain of trust — the technical core of PKI. At the top of the chain sits the Browser Trust Store: a list of roughly 150 Root CAs built into your browser or operating system — pre-installed by Apple, Microsoft, Mozilla etc. You trusted them the moment you bought your device. These Root CAs sign Intermediate CAs. And the Intermediates issue the actual certificates for websites — so-called Leaf certificates. Why the detour through Intermediates? Because the private key of a Root CA is so valuable that it lives offline in a Hardware Security Module — completely disconnected from the internet. The daily work is handled by Intermediates. And if an Intermediate gets compromised, you can revoke it without touching the Root. Timing: ~2 minutes.

What's Inside a Certificate?

X.509 Certificate (simplified)
Subject: CN = yourbank.de Issuer: Let's Encrypt R3 Valid from: 2026-03-12 Valid to: 2026-06-10 (90 days) Public Key: ECDSA P-256 (elliptic curve algorithm) SAN: yourbank.de ← Subject Alternative Names www.yourbank.de Serial: 03:4f:a1:… Signature: (signed by CA) ─────

The three most important fields

Subject Which domain was this certificate issued for? Browser checks: does this match the URL?
Public Key The server's public key — the centerpiece. Only whoever holds the matching private key can sign with it.
CA Signature This is where the CA vouches. The browser verifies this signature against the CA's public key — closing the chain.
This is what an X.509 certificate looks like — the format that's been in use for decades. In reality it contains even more fields, but these are the most important ones. Subject says: which domain was this certificate issued for? The browser checks that against the URL — if you visit "yourbank.de" but the certificate was issued for "evil.com", you get a warning. The Public Key is the heart of it: that's the bank's public key. Only the bank has the corresponding private key. Later in the handshake, the server has to produce a signature with it — and only whoever has that private key can do that. And the CA's Signature closes the chain: the CA signed this certificate with its private key. The browser can verify that using the CA's public key. If the signature checks out, the browser knows: this CA vouches for this domain. Timing: ~2 minutes. Then hand over to Tim.

TLS in Action

The Handshake — and when the system fails

Kargl Tim

Thanks, Paulina. I'm Tim, and I'll now show you how all of this comes together in practice — in the form of the TLS handshake. TLS 1.3 — the current version — does this in a matter of milliseconds. And then I'll tell you about a real-world case where the system broke down. Timing: ~15 seconds transition.

Cipher Suites

A cipher suite is a recipe — a named combination of algorithms that defines how a TLS connection is secured.
During the handshake, browser and server agree on one recipe to use for everything that follows.

Example — TLS 1.2
TLS _ ECDHE _ RSA _ WITH _ AES_256_GCM _ SHA384
① Key Exchange ② Auth ③ Encryption ④ Integrity

The next four slides explain each step — starting with the trickiest one.

Before the handshake, let's understand what a cipher suite actually is — because without this, the handshake won't make sense. Think of a cipher suite as a cooking recipe. It doesn't contain any food itself — it just tells you: use these exact tools and methods. Browser and server negotiate one recipe together during the handshake, and then follow it for everything. A cipher suite has four jobs. We'll go through each one on its own slide because they each deserve a proper explanation. The trickiest one is key exchange — so let's start there. Timing: ~30 seconds, just an intro.

① Key Exchange — ECDHE

How do browser and server agree on a shared secret — without ever sending it over the network?

The trick
Mixing colors is easy. Unmixing is nearly impossible.

In ECDHE, math on elliptic curves plays the role of color mixing — combining is trivial, reversing is computationally infeasible.
Ephemeral = key pairs are generated fresh for every session and thrown away after. Even if an attacker records traffic today and steals the server key tomorrow — past sessions stay safe.
This is the tricky one, so let's use an analogy. Imagine Alice and Bob want to agree on a shared secret color. But they're communicating over a public channel — Mallory can see everything they send. They start by agreeing on a common color — let's say yellow. That's public, Mallory sees it too. No problem. Now Alice picks a secret color — red. She mixes it with yellow and gets orange. She sends orange to Bob. Bob picks a secret color — blue. He mixes it with yellow and gets green. He sends green to Alice. Now here's the magic: Alice takes the green she received and adds her secret red — she gets a brownish color. Bob takes the orange he received and adds his secret blue — he gets the exact same brownish color. They both arrived at the same final color — without ever sending their secret color over the wire. Mallory saw yellow, orange, and green — but she can't unmix colors. She can't figure out what the final secret is. In ECDHE, math on elliptic curves does exactly what color mixing does here — combining is easy, reversing is computationally infeasible. And because the key pairs are ephemeral — generated fresh for every session — even if someone steals the server's key years later, they can't decrypt old sessions. That's Forward Secrecy. Timing: ~2 minutes.

② Authentication — RSA / ECDSA

How does the server prove it really is who it claims to be?

The key insight
A signature made with a private key can only be verified with the matching public key.

If the signature checks out → only the real server could have made it. Nobody else has that private key.
The public key comes from the certificate — which the CA signed. That's how the two mechanisms connect: PKI proves the public key belongs to the right domain, then the server uses it to prove it holds the private key.
Now we know how to establish a shared secret. But there's still a problem: how do we know we established that secret with the right server, and not with Mallory? That's what authentication solves. The server takes a message — basically "I am yourbank.de" — and signs it with its private key. The signature is a cryptographic operation: only someone with that specific private key could have produced it. The browser then takes that signature and verifies it using the public key from the certificate. If the signature matches — only the real server could have sent it. The private key never left the server. And here's where PKI connects to TLS: the public key the browser uses to verify comes from the certificate. And that certificate was signed by a CA the browser already trusts. So the whole chain holds together. Timing: ~1:30 minutes.

③ Bulk Encryption — AES-256-GCM

Now that both sides share a secret key — how is the actual data encrypted?

Why symmetric?
After key exchange, both sides have the same shared secret. Symmetric encryption uses that one key to both encrypt and decrypt — it's much faster than asymmetric crypto, making it practical for large amounts of data.
AES-256 = 256-bit key — more combinations than atoms in the observable universe. GCM mode also provides integrity — so steps ③ and ④ are handled together in one pass.
After the key exchange and authentication, both sides now have the same shared secret. Now they can actually encrypt the data. They use AES — Advanced Encryption Standard — with a 256-bit key. It's a symmetric algorithm: the same key encrypts and decrypts. Symmetric crypto is much faster than the asymmetric stuff we used in key exchange — that's why we use it here for the bulk of the data. The GCM mode is particularly nice because it handles both encryption and integrity in one operation — so steps three and four of the cipher suite are actually handled together. AES-256 is considered computationally unbreakable with current technology. A 256-bit key has more possible combinations than there are atoms in the observable universe. Timing: ~1 minute.

④ Integrity — SHA-384

How does the receiver know the message wasn't tampered with in transit?

How it works
A hash function takes any input and produces a fixed-length fingerprint. Any change — even one character — produces a completely different hash.

Both sides compute the hash. If they match → data is intact. If not → rejected.
In AES-GCM, encryption and integrity are combined — the MAC is computed automatically. There's no separate step needed in TLS 1.3.
The last piece: how do we know nobody tampered with a message in transit? A hash function takes any input — a word, a sentence, a whole file — and produces a fixed-size fingerprint. SHA-384 always produces exactly 48 bytes, no matter what the input is. The critical property: change even one character — like the "o" in "Hello" to a zero — and the hash is completely different. Not slightly different. Completely different. There's no pattern, no way to predict it. So when the browser receives a message, it computes the hash of what it received and compares it to the hash the server sent. If they match, nothing was changed. If they don't match, something was tampered with and the message gets rejected immediately. As I mentioned — in AES-GCM mode, this is built in. Encryption and integrity checking happen in one operation. So in practice, TLS 1.3 handles steps three and four together. Timing: ~1 minute.

TLS 1.3 — The Handshake

Browser (Client) Server (yourbank.de)
ClientHello List of supported cipher suites + temporary public key (ECDHE)
ServerHello + Certificate Public key + complete certificate chain
CertificateVerify + Finished Signature proves: I hold the private key
Finished Browser verified everything → connection established
Browser verifies
Certificate chain  ·  Hostname  ·  Validity period  ·  Signature  ·  Revocation status (OCSP — checks if cert was revoked early)
Forward Secrecy
Session key derived from ephemeral (one-time, never reused) ECDHE keys — traffic recorded today stays unreadable even in 10 years.
Alright, now we see the handshake — the heart of TLS 1.3. The nice thing: TLS 1.3 dramatically simplified the handshake compared to TLS 1.2. In practice, all of this takes just a few milliseconds. Step 1: The browser sends a ClientHello — a list of supported cipher suites and a temporary public key for key exchange (ECDHE). Step 2: The server responds with ServerHello and sends its certificate — the complete chain from Leaf to Root. Step 3: The server sends CertificateVerify — a signature produced with its private key. This is the crucial proof: only whoever holds the private key can do this. And the private key never leaves the server. Step 4: The browser sends Finished — it has checked everything, the connection is up. What exactly does the browser check? The certificate chain, the hostname, whether the certificate is still valid, whether it's been revoked, and the CA's signature. And the cool feature of TLS 1.3 — Forward Secrecy: session keys are generated from temporary, ephemeral keys. Even if someone records all the traffic today and later steals the server's private key, they cannot decrypt those old sessions. The session keys only existed for that one moment in time. Timing: ~3 minutes — this is the most important slide.

When the System Gets Fooled

PKI is not invulnerable — the weakest link is the CAs themselves.

Case Study: DigiNotar, 2011
Dutch CA was hacked. Attackers — allegedly Iranian authorities — issued real, valid certificates for *.google.com.

They used these to intercept the Gmail accounts of hundreds of thousands of Iranian citizens — the browser showed no warning, everything looked legitimate.

Outcome: All major browsers removed DigiNotar from their trust stores within days. The company went bankrupt.
The Answer: Certificate Transparency
Mandatory since 2018 for all browser-accepted certificates: every newly issued certificate must appear in public, append-only logs — immutably.

Domain owners can continuously monitor whether unknown certificates have been issued for their domain. Any mis-issuance becomes instantly visible.

Tool: crt.sh — public search across CT logs.
Further risks covered on the next slide →
PKI is a very robust system — but it's not infallible. The weakest point is the trust placed in CAs themselves. What happens when a CA gets compromised? In 2011, DigiNotar, a Dutch CA, was hacked. The attackers — attributed to Iranian intelligence — didn't just steal data. They used the infrastructure to issue real, cryptographically valid certificates for *.google.com. And with those, they intercepted the traffic of hundreds of thousands of Iranian Gmail users. The browser showed a green padlock — everything looked legitimate. When it came to light, Chrome, Firefox, and Internet Explorer removed DigiNotar from their trust stores within days. That was the end of the company. The response to DigiNotar and similar incidents is called Certificate Transparency — CT. Since 2018, every certificate that browsers should accept must be logged in public, immutable logs. Domain owners can continuously check: has someone issued a certificate for my domain that I don't know about? If so, that's a red flag. Anyone can do this with crt.sh — just enter a domain and see what certificates exist. Timing: ~2 minutes.

Further Risks

PKI and TLS are robust — but no system is perfect.

Risk 1
Stolen Server Keys
If an attacker steals a server's private key, they can impersonate that server — even with a valid certificate.

Mitigation: Short validity periods (90 days with Let's Encrypt), regular key rotation, and certificate revocation (OCSP).
Risk 2
CA Mis-issuance
A CA accidentally issues a certificate for a domain to the wrong person — through human error or a flawed validation process, not necessarily a hack.

Mitigation: Certificate Transparency logs catch mis-issued certificates within hours.
Risk 3
Downgrade Attacks
An attacker tries to force both sides to use an older, weaker version of TLS (e.g., TLS 1.0) with known vulnerabilities.

Mitigation: TLS 1.3 removed all legacy options entirely — no weak fallback exists anymore.
Okay, before we wrap up — three more risks worth knowing about, even if they're less dramatic than DigiNotar. First: stolen server keys. A certificate proves identity — but only as long as the private key stays secret. If an attacker gets hold of it, they can impersonate the server perfectly. That's why certificates have short lifetimes — 90 days with Let's Encrypt — so even a stolen key becomes useless quickly. Second: CA mis-issuance. This doesn't require a hack. Sometimes a CA makes a mistake in their validation process and issues a certificate to the wrong person. It's rare but happens. Again, Certificate Transparency catches this — the mis-issued cert shows up in the public log and the domain owner gets alerted. Third: downgrade attacks. Older TLS versions had real weaknesses. An attacker in the middle could try to force both sides to negotiate an old version — say TLS 1.0 — and then exploit a known vulnerability. TLS 1.3 fixed this by simply removing all the old, weak options. There's no fallback to exploit. Timing: ~1 minute.
